# Bloom levels, verbs, and marks

Use this to keep the blueprint's `bloom` field honest and to keep item stems
consistent with the level they claim.

| Level | Typical marks | Stem verbs | The student must |
|---|---|---|---|
| remember | 1–2 | define, list, name, state, identify | Retrieve a fact verbatim |
| understand | 1–3 | explain, describe, summarise, classify, compare | Restate in their own words |
| apply | 3–5 | calculate, solve, demonstrate, use, determine | Run a known procedure on new inputs |
| analyze | 5–8 | differentiate, examine, deduce, why does | Break a whole into parts and see relations |
| evaluate | 5–10 | justify, critique, argue, assess, which is better | Judge against criteria and defend it |
| create | 8–15 | design, propose, formulate, construct | Assemble something not in the material |

## Common mislabels

**"Explain" is not always understand.** "Explain the water cycle" is understand.
"Explain why the reaction rate doubled when temperature rose 10°C" is analyze —
the student must connect two ideas the material presented separately.

**Numericals are not automatically apply.** A numerical where the student
substitutes into a stated formula is apply. One where the student must first
decide which of three formulas applies is analyze.

**Create is rare and expensive.** Most papers should have zero or one create-level
question. It cannot be marked objectively and it eats time.

## Marks-to-time heuristic

Roughly 1.5 minutes per mark for a median student, plus 5 minutes of reading time
for the paper as a whole. A 50-mark paper wants about 80 minutes. If the
blueprint's time estimate exceeds the stated duration by more than 15%, cut marks
rather than cutting question count — students panic at a paper that looks long,
but they fail one that *is* long.

## Distractor sources for MCQ

Good distractors come from somewhere specific. Pull from:

- The most common arithmetic error for that operation (sign flip, off-by-factor-of-ten)
- A definition from an adjacent concept in the same chapter
- The answer to a subtly different question the student might think was asked
- A partially completed calculation (correct through step 2, stopped early)

A distractor invented at random is worse than no question at all — it teaches the
student that guessing works.
